Cura vs PrusaSlicer vs Bambu Studio: Which Slicer Should You Use?

Your slicer is the software that turns a 3D model into the instructions your printer follows. Choosing the right one makes a real difference - especially when you are learning. This guide compares the three most popular free slicers so you can pick the one that suits your printer and workflow.

What Is a Slicer and Why Does It Matter?

A slicer takes your STL or 3MF file and converts it into G-code: the layer-by-layer instructions that tell your printer exactly where to move, how fast, and how much filament to push. Different slicers generate slightly different G-code, have different interfaces, and are better suited to different printers.

The slicer affects print quality, print speed, support generation, and how much time you spend fiddling with settings. Getting the right one for your setup matters.

The Three Main Options

Cura

Developed by Ultimaker, now maintained as open source. Free to download and use with any printer.

Best for: Creality Ender 3, CR-10, and most budget FDM printers. Also works well with a huge range of other brands.

Strengths:

  • Massive user base - more tutorials, forum posts, and help available than any other slicer
  • Excellent plugin ecosystem - tree supports, variable layer height, and many quality-of-life features are available as plugins
  • Works with virtually every printer brand
  • Profile presets for hundreds of printers built in

Weaknesses:

  • Interface can feel overwhelming at first - there are a lot of settings
  • Slower to slice complex models than some alternatives
  • Default profiles are sometimes conservative (not always the best starting point)

Who should use it: Ender 3 and Creality users, people who want the largest community for support, anyone who prints on multiple different printers.

Download Cura free from Ultimaker

PrusaSlicer

Developed by Prusa Research, based on the open-source Slic3r project. Free to download and works with any printer, not just Prusas.

Best for: Prusa printers (MK4, Mini, XL) but also excellent for any printer where you want clean, reliable results.

Strengths:

  • Cleaner, less cluttered interface than Cura - easier to learn
  • Excellent built-in support generation, including organic tree supports
  • Strong variable layer height tools built in (no plugin needed)
  • Very reliable default profiles for Prusa printers
  • Active development with regular meaningful updates

Weaknesses:

  • Slightly smaller community than Cura, so fewer third-party tutorials
  • Printer profiles for non-Prusa brands are less polished than Cura's

Who should use it: Prusa users (obviously), but also anyone who finds Cura too cluttered and wants a cleaner starting point. PrusaSlicer is genuinely excellent for any FDM printer.

Download PrusaSlicer free from Prusa

Bambu Studio

Developed by Bambu Lab for their own printers, but available as open source and usable with other printers via the "Generic" profiles.

Best for: Bambu Lab printers (A1, A1 Mini, P1S, X1C). Works poorly for non-Bambu printers compared to the alternatives above.

Strengths:

  • Best-in-class integration with Bambu printers - print directly, monitor via camera, AMS filament management all built in
  • Excellent default profiles that are actually well-tuned
  • Multi-colour and multi-material support is class-leading
  • Clean, modern interface

Weaknesses:

  • Very limited usefulness outside Bambu printers
  • Less customisable than Cura or PrusaSlicer for advanced users
  • Community is smaller and newer

Who should use it: Bambu Lab printer owners. If you have a Bambu printer, use Bambu Studio - it is clearly the best choice. If you do not own a Bambu, use Cura or PrusaSlicer instead.

Which One Should You Download First?

You have a Bambu Lab printer: Use Bambu Studio. It is purpose-built for your printer and everything works better with it.

You have a Prusa printer: Use PrusaSlicer. The profiles are excellent and the interface is clean.

You have a Creality (Ender 3, CR-10, etc.) or other budget printer: Start with Cura. The profile library is enormous and the community support is unmatched.

You are unsure or want to try both: Download PrusaSlicer first - the interface is easier to learn on, and switching to Cura later is straightforward once you understand the concepts.

You are not locked in to your choice. Most experienced users have at least two slicers installed and use different ones for different jobs.

Tips for Getting Started With Any Slicer

Use the built-in printer profiles. All three slicers have presets for popular printers. Start with the preset for your printer before changing anything.

Change one setting at a time. When you are learning, changing multiple settings at once makes it impossible to know what actually improved your print.

Save your own profiles. Once you have a combination of settings that works well, save it as a named profile so you do not have to recreate it.

The slicer recommender tool on our tools site can help you pick based on your printer and experience level if you are still not sure.
